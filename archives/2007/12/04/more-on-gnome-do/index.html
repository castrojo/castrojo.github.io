    More on GNOME Do.

    I hate to sound like a broken record, but the more I follow GNOME Do as a project, the more I get excited about the usage of open source in academia. Not in the typical “our department uses Linux and Apache” kind of way, but in a software development / learning to be a computer scientist kind of way.

    GNOME Do is a senior design project by some students at the University of Pennsylvania. One of the maintainers, David Siegel, had moved over to Ubuntu from OSX and had a desire to have something like Quicksilver for GNOME. Rather than doing things the traditional senior design way, they decided to make their project Open Source from the beginning[1]:

    “GNOME Do is an open source project, which means that all specifications, source code, documentation, and other project resources are publicly available for anyone to read or compile at any stage in our development process.”

    and …

    “This approach is a double-edged sword, however: by inviting people to scrutinize and interact with us and our work, the quality and thoroughness of our project should be higher, but managing community interaction adds a large, organizational dimension to our project that most senior design projects do not have to deal with.”

    (Emphasis mine). I think this is the most important lesson learned here. Learning how to manage volunteers is pretty difficult to do (think: herding cats), so I would argue that this kind of experience is just as important as the hacking itself. Of course, it does help when you get help:

    “Two great challenges will be learning to use the necessary tools and libraries … To help bring us up to speed with these aspects of our project, Sean Egan, the lead maintainer of Pidgin … has agreed to mentor us.”

    !! Not a bad way to finish out your senior year of computer science. On a lighter note, one of their Calculus(!) professors has even submitted a patch.

    When talking to David on the phone we discussed about how useful Launchpad is for a project like this. They didn’t have to mess around with setting up a bug tracker, SVN, find hosting, or any of that noise. They were able to just register their project and get to work almost immediately.

    Rick Harding has a PPA for ubuntu users, (Info here) and David Nielsen has taken care of the Fedora packages.
