    Music Geeks and Computer Geeks

    Like many musical amateurs, I often find myself justifying new equipment as an “investment” on becoming a better player. This usually isn’t a fiscally responsible viewpoint though … in fact, it doesn’t really pass the common sense test either. If I really wanted to be a better bassist, I would practice more.

    But like computer geeks, I think music geeks dig gear. After all, I don’t need a Core 2 Duo in my computer, but I like having it.

    I’ve been really happy with my “starter” bass. I had played in jazz band in high school (and actually learned on a double bass), and about 6 months ago I wanted to pick up the bass again. I picked up an Ibanez RD500 Roadgear, which is just a great bass, Jono seemed to like it:

    I really enjoy the neck on this bass. It’s very quick, but has a neat throwback head that has a classic Fender look, without the 2x4 feeling I get when I play a real Fender. In the hands of an accomplished bassist, like Barry Warsaw, it’s a monster of awesome.

    However, I like to have fun when I play. I like something with some personality. This is why I picked up an Epiphone Thunderbird IV Goth:

    I spent all day today practicing, and while I find myself sounding better with the Ibanez (by a long shot), I still pick up the Epiphone first. It has some flaws; the neck dives a bunch, and it’s pretty lame that Epiphone/Gibson does this whole “Goth” marketing crap, when really I just want something all black. It doesn’t sound as nice as the Ibanez, and the neck is pretty kludgy.

    But still, I feel like I identify with the Thunderbird more. There’s something stupidly primal about “personality”. James Hetfield is famous for his ESP Explorer. Dave Mustaine wouldn’t be “King Dave” without a King V. Roger Waters wouldn’t look right in anything but a P-Bass. Geddy Lee has a signature line with Fender, but everyone always thinks of Rickenbacker when he comes up in conversation.

    I think this kind of thinking is pretty weird. Sort of like when you go to a LUG and there’s always people arguing about programming languages, operating systems, version control systems, etc. Lots of people always get bent out of shape about these kind of things, but there’s always that one guy that doesn’t really care, he just rocks at all of them.

    I suspect that great musicians are like great programmers, the tools don’t really matter TOO MUCH, it’s all about rocking out. I think we all get bent out of shape about stupid things, like programming language or other implementation details. But at the same time, I don’t really care what strings Dave is using on the latest Megadeth album, I just want it to rock.

    NOTE: This post brought on by two things:

    a) A commercial I just saw on VH-1. It’s a talk with Alice Cooper on how he meets all these young bands, and they tell him “Oh wow man, we have the attitude” and all this stuff, and at the end he just asks “Where’s the song?”

    b) David Siegel’s response to criticisms about his platform of choice.
