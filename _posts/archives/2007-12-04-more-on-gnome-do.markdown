I hate to sound like a [broken
record](http://stompbox.typepad.com/blog/2007/11/gnome-do.html), but the
more I follow [GNOME Do](http://launchpad.net/gc) as a project, the more
I get excited about the usage of open source in academia. Not in the
typical “our department uses Linux and Apache” kind of way, but in a
software development / learning to be a computer scientist kind of way.

GNOME Do is a senior design project by some students at the [University
of Pennsylvania](http://www.cis.upenn.edu/). One of the maintainers,
David Siegel, had moved over to Ubuntu from OSX and had a desire to have
something like Quicksilver for GNOME. Rather than doing things the
traditional senior design way, they decided to make their project Open
Source from the beginning\[1\]:

> “GNOME Do is an open source project, which means that all
> specifications, source code, documentation, and other project
> resources are publicly available for anyone to read or compile at any
> stage in our development process.”

and …

> “This approach is a double-edged sword, however: by inviting people to
> scrutinize and interact with us and our work, the quality and
> thoroughness of our project should be higher, but *managing community
> interaction adds a large, organizational dimension to our project that
> most senior design projects do not have to deal with.*”

(Emphasis mine). I think this is the most important lesson learned here.
Learning how to manage volunteers is pretty difficult to do (think:
herding cats), so I would argue that this kind of experience is just as
important as the hacking itself. Of course, it does help when you get
help:

> “Two great challenges will be learning to use the necessary tools and
> libraries … To help bring us up to speed with these aspects of our
> project, Sean Egan, the lead maintainer of Pidgin … has agreed to
> mentor us.”

!! Not a bad way to finish out your senior year of computer science. On
a lighter note, one of their Calculus(!) professors has even
[submitted](https://bugs.edge.launchpad.net/gc/+bug/173505) a patch.

When talking to David on the phone we discussed about how useful
Launchpad is for a project like this. They didn’t have to mess around
with setting up a bug tracker, SVN, find hosting, or any of that noise.
They were able to just register their project and get to work almost
immediately.

Rick Harding has a PPA for ubuntu users, ([Info
here](https://edge.launchpad.net/gc/)) and David Nielsen has taken care
of the [Fedora
packages](http://koji.fedoraproject.org/koji/packageinfo?packageID=5289).

\[1\] [GNOME Do Status
report](http://groups.google.com/group/gnome-do/attach/b5f6ff7a69cad518/GNOME%20Do%20Project%20Update.pdf?part=2)
